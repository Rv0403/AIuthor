Personal Finance Basics — Reference Corpus

Emergency funds should cover three to six months of essential living expenses. This buffer protects against job loss and unexpected medical bills.

Compound interest works in your favor when you invest early. Starting at age 25 versus 35 can mean hundreds of thousands more at retirement due to time in market.

The 50/30/20 budget rule suggests allocating fifty percent of after-tax income to needs, thirty percent to wants, and twenty percent to savings and debt repayment.

High-interest credit card debt above eight percent APR should typically be prioritized before aggressive investing, because guaranteed "returns" from paying debt often exceed average market gains.

Diversification across asset classes — stocks, bonds, cash — reduces portfolio volatility without necessarily sacrificing long-term returns.

Tax-advantaged accounts like 401(k) and IRA offer deferred or tax-free growth; employer matching is an immediate return on contributions.

Inflation erodes purchasing power; cash-heavy portfolios may lose real value over decades. Equities historically outpace inflation over long horizons.

Dollar-cost averaging invests fixed amounts on a schedule, reducing timing risk and emotional decision-making during market swings.

Insurance transfers catastrophic risk: health, disability, term life, and property coverage are foundational before optimizing investments.

Net worth equals assets minus liabilities. Tracking monthly helps reveal spending leaks and progress toward financial independence.
